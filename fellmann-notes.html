Chapter 1: Some Background Basics

Good morning!  I am so happy you are joining us for our Voyage of Discovery in the wonderful world of Human Geography!  Human Geography is the study of man's interconnections with the physical Earth.  Basically, we will be learning where places and people are and why they are there!

This year we will be answering questions such as: what basic concepts do geographers use to study the world?  Where are people located throughout the world? What different cultural groups make up the world and why are these differences important?  How do people earn a living in different parts of the world?  Why are cities and other urban cities important and how are they different? Of course, we will look at many other concepts, too!

Geography is a spatial science.  Geographers study space to locate where people live and why they choose to live in that place.  We are all born geographers!  We all have a sense of curiosity about the spaces and places around us!

I conducted a study of Old Order Amish children’s concepts of their neighborhood and compared them with rural non-Amish children who lived in the same area.  Because of the cultural differences between Amish and non-Amish kids, their sense of place as shown in sketch maps of their neighborhoods, was really interesting and not at all similar!

The Amish children only drew their immediate farm or house and barn yard.  The non-Amish children drew maps of the entire rural community. What I discovered was that much can be learned about an individual's perception of place, neighborhood, home, from their sketch maps! Home and family are the Amish child's entire world (they are very sheltered from the n on-Amish world) while the non-Amish child  has a much larger neighborhood thanks to exposure through T.V., radio, movies, newspapers, and other media.

This chapter introduces you to a few of the important concepts regarding space and place – the two core concepts of geography.  You will find out why a farmer lives and farms in a certain location  and why he probably won’t want to live and farm in another.

First you will learn about a sense of place and its opposite – placelessness.  What makes your city special?  You will learn about location and the difference between absolute and relative locations.   Site and situation relate directly to location.  Distance is another concept we explore this week.

How do geographers describe where things are? Why is each point on Earth unique?  How are different locations interrelated? How do people relate to their environment?

 

- Geographers study space to locate where people live and why they choose to live in that place; they are interested in how things are interrelated in different regions and give evidence of the existence of “spatial systems”; they focus on the interaction of people and social groups with their environment and with each other; they seek to understand how and why physical and cultural spatial patterns evolved through time and continue to change

- Geography is not simply knowing where things are; it is the study of spatial variation, of how and why things differ from place to place on the surface of the earth; it is further the study of how observable spatial patterns evolved through time; Geography is about geographic space and its content; it is the mother of sciences; is the study of the earth’s surface and its physical and cultural content

- Knowing where things are located geographically is only the first step toward understanding why things are where they are, and what events and processes determine or change their distribution

- Because geographers study both the physical environment and the human use of environment, they are sensitive to the variety of forces affecting a place and to the interactions among them

- The name geography was coined by Greek scientist Eratosthenes over 2200 years ago from the words geo “the earth”, and graphein “to write”. That writing focused on the physical structure of the earth and the nature and activities of the people who inhabited it

- To Strabo the task of geography was to “describe the several parts of the inhabited world….to write the assessment of the countries of the world and to treat the differences between countries”

- Greek and later Roman geographers measured the earth, devised the global grid of parallels and meridians (latitudes and longitudes) and drew on that grid maps of their known world; they focused on how humans lived, similarities and differences in cultures, how they used the land

- Strabo warned against the idea that humans were determined by the physical environment; he said that humans were part of a human environmental partnership

- ancient Chinese were also involved (500 A.D); as Christian Europe entered Middle Ages and lost greek and roman geographical work (1400 A.D.), Muslim scholars retained knowledge and described and analyzed their known world in “Roger’s Book.” (written by arab geographer Idrisi directed by Roger II(1099 - 1154), to collect all known geographical info and assemble it in an accurate representation of the world; divided into seven climates of greek geographers, which were each divided by perpendicular lines into 11 parts beginning with the west coast of Africa and ending with the east coast of Asia)

- Modern geography surged in 17th century- outset was recognized as a broadly based integrative study

- 19th century- national censuses, trade statistics, ethnographic studies gave foundation to human geography

- Geography is characterized by three interests:

  1. Areal variation of physical and human phenomena on the surface of the earth- examine relationship between human societies and the natural environments they occupy
  2. Focus on the spatial systems that link physical phenomena and human activities in one area of the earth
  3. Regional analysis: geography studies human-environmental- ecological- relationships and spatial systems in specific locational settings- REgional geography

- Others identify classes of things, rather than segments of the earth’s surface: Systematic Geographers- focus their attentions on one or a few related aspects of the physical environment or of human population and society; in each case, the topic selected is examined in its interrelationships with other spatial systems; Physical geography directs its attention to the natural environmental side of the human; the other systematic branch is Human geography

- Human Geography: deals with the world as it is and the world as it might be; emphasis on people- where they are, what they are like, how they interact what kinds of landscapes they use; it provides integration for all the social sciences- it gives them the necessary spatial and systems viewpoint; human geography helps us understand the world we occupy and appreciate the circumstances affecting people and countries; it clarifies contrasts in societies

- 8 Subdivisions of human geography:

Political Geography- political science; Population Geography- demography; Historical Geography- History; Urban Geography- urban studies, planning; Social Geography- Sociology, language, religious studies; Economic Geography- regional economics, economics; Behavior Geography- psychology, economics; Cultural GEography- Anthropology, sociology, history

- The topics of human geography are diverse, but that emphasizes the fact that all geographers are united by the questions they ask: what, where, how did it come to be, where in relation to other things, how is it changing, how is it functioning as a whole, how do people affect it, how does its location affect people? These questions are rooted in Geography’s central concern with Space and Place

- Space implies areal extent and may be understood in both an absolute and relative sense; Absolute space- physically real with measurable extent and definable boundaries- therefore space is fundamental to areal relationships among physical or cultural features on the earth’s surface and is basic to geographic interests like making maps, analyzing distributions, and conducting spatial analysis; Relative space- perceptual and variable- therefore space can be seen as socially produced, reflecting activities and interrelationships; since these two are constantly changing, relative space adjusts in response

- Place is the companion of Space. Place is a synonym for location. Place refers to the attributes and values we associate with a location.

- The sense of place is reinforced by local and regional distinctiveness. It may be diminished and replaced by Placelessness.

- Geography is a spatial science, concerned with spatial behavior and spatial relationships, with spatial interactions, with spatial processes. The word spatial always carries the idea of the way items are distributed, the way movements occur, and the way processes operate over the whole or a part of the surface of the earth.

- The need to understand these things frame the questions geographers ask.

- Means by which Geographers express fundamental observations about the earth:

  1. Places have location, direction, and distance with respect to other places- spatial concepts

These are ways of assessing the space around us and identifying our position in relation to other items and places of interest. They are also essential in understanding the processes of spatial interaction

  • Location: the location is the starting point of all geographic study as well as personal movements and spatial actions. We think and refer to location in 2 different senses: absolute and relative

- Absolute location is the identification of place by some precise and accepted system of coordinates; sometimes called mathematical location. We have several such systems of pinpointing positions- parallels and meridians- the location of any point can be described by reference of latitude and longitude. This is unique to each place, and has value in measuring distance or finding directions.

- When geographers say that location matters, they are not talking about absolute but relative location- the position of a place in relation to other places and activities. This expresses spatial interconnection and may carry social and economic implications.

- Geography uses these to make distinction between Site and Situation of a place:

- Site, an absolute location, refers to the physical and cultural characteristics and attributes of the place itself. It is more than mathematical location b/c it tells us something about the internal features of that place

- Situation refers to the external relations of a locale; it is an expression of relative location with particular reference to items of significance

  • Direction: is a second universal spatial concept; it has more than one meaning and can be expressed in absolute and relative terms

- Absolute direction is based on cardinal points of N, S, E, and W; they are derived from nature- the rising and setting of the sun for E and W, and the sky location of noontime sun and stars for N and S.

- Relative or relational directions- out West, back East, down South, Near East, Far East- though these reference cardinal compass points, they are culturally based

  • Distance: joins location and direction as a commonly understood term that has dual meanings- absolute and relative

- Absolute distance refers to the spatial separation between two points on the earth’s surface measured by a standard unit such as miles or kilometers

- Relative distance transforms linear measurements into other units more meaningful for the space relationship; most people think of time, money, or psychological transformation rather than linear distance (eg. 20 min. walk instead of 2 miles; 10 dollar cab away instead of 20 miles, and dangerous late night walk back to car or first time trip)

- Lines of equal travel time (isochrones: from greek isos- equal, and chronos- time); distance relationships, their measurements, and their meaning for human spatial interaction are fundamental to our understanding of human geography

  1. A place has size; it may be large or small. Scale is important.

- When we say a place is large or small, we speak both of the nature of the place and of the generalizations that can be made

 

  • Size

 

  • Scale- can be a feature of every map and essential to recognizing the areal meaning of the map; implies the degree of generalization represented; geographic inquiry can occur at many different size scales
  1. A place has both physical structure and cultural content.

- Geographers are concerned with identifying and analyzing the details of attributes and recognizing the interrelationship between the physical and cultural components of area: the human- environmental interface

  • Physical characteristics- natural aspects provide the setting within which human action occurs- they help shape, but do not dictate, how humans live; human activity has been so universal that no “natural landscape” exists any longer
  • Environmental circumstances- directly affect agricultural potential and reliability, indirectly them may influence such matters as employment patterns, trade flows, population distributions
  • Cultural Landscape- visible expression of human activity

- Although the focus of this book is on human characteristics, geographers are aware that physical content of an area is important in understanding activity patterns and interconnections between people and their environments

  1. The attributes of places develop and change over time.

- characteristics of places today are the result of constantly changing past conditions; to understand fully the nature and development of places, geographers must view places as the present result of the past operation of distinctive physical and cultural processes; “How did it come to be what and where it is?” This is an inquiry about process and about becoming; the forces and events shaping the physical and explaining the cultural environment of places today are an important focus of geography; to understand them is to appreciate more fully the changing human spatial order of the world

  1. The elements of places interrelate with other places.

- relative location and distance lead to a fundamental spatial reality- places interact with other places in structured and comprehensible ways; in describing this Spatial Interaction, geographers use Accessibility and Connectivity

- Tobler’s First Law of Geography- in a spatial sense everything is related but relationships are stronger when items are nearer; interaction between places diminishes in intensity and frequency as distance between them increases- Distance Decay

  • Accessibility- How easy is it to surmount the barrier of time and space separation- friction of distance
  • Connectivity- broader concept implying tangible and intangible ways in which places are connected; when routes are fixed, networks determine the efficiency of movement and connectedness of points; technology has erased time and distance barriers and have reduced our dependence on physical movement
  • Spatial diffusion- process of dispersion of an idea or an item from a center of origin to more distant points; diffusion rates are affected by distance and population, means of communication, and prestige of the originating node

- Expansion diffusion: Hierarchical diffusion – king to people, Contagious diffusion – rapid spread of idea or trait into area like influenza, Stimulus diffusion – spread of a big idea or innovative feature

  • Globalization- stemmed from the above, recognition that spatial interaction is a fundamental organizing principle of life; implies increasing interconnection of peoples and societies as the full range of processes become international
  1. The content of places is structured and explainable.

- How are objects distributed in area. Where is it located, how does the location relate to other things, how did it come to be- these questions carry the conviction that the contents of an area are arranged or structured- called spatial distribution; can be analyzed by three elements

  • Density- measure of the number or quantity of anything within a defined unit of area; it is not simply a count of items but of items in relation to the space in which they are found

- WHen the relationship is absolute, it is arithmetic density; When it is a measure of number of persons per unit area, it is physiological density

  • Dispersion/concentration/distribution) is a statement of the amount of spread of a phenomenon over an area; it tells not how many but how far things are spread; if they are close spatially, they are clustered or agglomerated; if they are spread out, they are dispersed or scattered
  • -Pattern the geometric arrangement of objects in space- refers to distribution, emphasizes design

- The distribution of houses along a street is linear; items concentrated around a single node is called centralized; an unstructured irregular distribution is called random; most streets in america display a grid or rectilinear pattern; a hexagonal pattern is a mainstay of central place theory

  1. Places may be generalized into regions of similarities and differences.

- the distinctive characteristics of a place suggests 2 ideas: first- no two places can be exactly the same; because geography is a spatial science, the inevitable uniqueness of place would seem to impose impossible problems of generalization; second- physical and cultural content show patterns of spatial similarity; these similarities permit us to recognize regions-earth areas that display significant elements of internal uniformity and external difference

- regions are devised- spatial summaries designed to bring order to the diversity of the world; at their root, they are based on recognition and mapping of spatial distributions

Types of Regions:

  1. Formal or uniform Region: uniformity in one or a limited combination of physical or cultural features (eg. Bible Belt)
  2. Functional or Nodal Region: parts are interdependent, operates as a dynamic, organizational unit; it has unity not only in the sense of static content but in the manner of its operational connectivity; its has a core area in which features are most clear; they lessen toward the margin or periphery
  3. Perceptual Regions: reflect feelings and images rather than data; the vernacular regions they recognize have reality in their minds and are reflected in regionally based names or slogans

- Maps are tools to identify regions and to analyze their content. The spatial distributions, patterns, and relations of interest to geographers usually cannot be easily observed and interpreted in the landscape itself. Only the map can reduce spatial distributions and interactions to an observable scale.

- Maps generalize the date they display; Scale- the relationship between size or length of a feature- determines that generalization

- the smaller the scale, the larger the area the map covers and the more generalized the data

- the larger the scale, the smaller the area and the more accurate representation

- the larger the earth area on a map, the bigger the distortion b/c a map has to depict a 3d Earth on a 2d paper

- Projection: the method chosen to represent the earth’s curved surface as a flat map; to minimize the distortion of the 4 main map properties- area, shape, distance, or direction

- The key reference points in the grid system are the N and S poles and equator and the prime meridian; latitude measures distance N and S of the equator, and parallels of latitude run east-west; Longitude is the angular distance E or W of the prime meridian and depicted by NS lines called meridians, which converge at the poles

- Properties of globe grid:

  1. All meridians are semicircles of equal length, each is one half the length of the equator.
  2. All meridians converge at the poles and are true north south semicircles
  3. All circles of latitude (parallels) are parallel to the equator and to each  other
  4. Parallels decrease in circumference as one nears the poles.
  5. Meridians and parallels intersect at right angles
  6. The scale on the surface of the globe is the same in every direction

- Only the globe grid retains all of these; to project it onto a flat surface distorts some of these properties

- Scale is reported to the map user in 3 ways: Verbal scale (1 in. to 1 mile, in words), representative fraction (how many linear units on the earth’s surface represented by one unit on the ma), and graphic scale (line or bar marked off in map units but labeled in ground units)

- Geographers are more interested in the depiction of spatial data and analysis of the patterns those data present. They must first select the items of concern to the problem, then decide how to display them for study or demonstration. THey can choose among different types of maps and different systems of symbolization

- Major classes of maps

  1. Familiar to everyone: General-purpose, Reference, or Location maps: their purpose is to show without analysis or interpretation a variety of natural or human made features of an area
  2. Thematic Maps: a map of any scale that presents a graphic theme
  • Qualitative: to show the distribution of a particular class of info- eg. location of oil fields, distribution of national parks, areas of agricultural specialization; interest is in where things are, not in numbers
  • Quantitative : show the spatial characteristic of numerical data; types of these maps:
  1. Graduated circle map- circles of different size to show the frequency of occurrence of a topic in different places; the larger the circle, the more frequent the incidence
  2. Dot map- single or specified number of occurrences of the item studied is recorded by a single dot; serves not only to record data but to suggest spatial pattern, distribution, and dispersion
  3. Isometric map- features lines (isolines) that connect points registering equal values of the item mapped (iso means equal); isotherms shown on daily weather maps connect points recording the same temperature during the day; identical elevations may be shown by contour lines
  4. Isopleth map- the calculation refers not to a point but to an areal statistic- persons per square kilo or average percentage of cropland- the isoline connects average values for unit areas
  5. Choropleth map- presents average value per preexisting areal unit- dwelling unit rents or assessed value by city block; when this map is based on the absolute number of items rather than on areal averaging, a misleading statement about density may be conveyed
  6. Statistical map: record the actual numbers or occurrences of the mapped item
  7. Cartogram: uses such statistical data to transform territorial space so that the largest areal unit is the one showing the greatest statistical value

- The growth and advancement of these three interrelated geospatial technologies has revolutionized geography and increased the ability to collect, analyze, and visually represent geographic data

  1. Global positioning systems- GPS- relies on 24 orbiting satellites, earth bound tracking stations that control them, and portable receivers that determine exact geographic locations based on signals from the satellites.
  2. Remote Sensing- new term, but process of detecting the nature of an object and content from a distance is more than 150 years old (ogitigraogs); early 20th century- fixed wing aircraft provided a platform for camera and photographer, by 1930s aerial photography from planned routes and positions permitted reliable data for large and small area mapping; advantages of satellites- speed, coverage, large regions, equipped to record and report back to earth digitized information from multiple parts of the electromagnetic spectrum, enable us to map the invisible, including atmospheric and weather conditions, provides images with applications in agriculture and forest inventory

- Satellite imagery is relayed by electronic signals to receiving stations, where computers convert them into photolike images

  1.           Geographic Information Systems- GIS- extends the use of digitized data and computer manipulation to investigate and display spatial information; a set of discrete informational overlays linked by reference to a basic locational grid of latitude and longitude; the system then permits the separate display of the spatial information contained in the database; allows the user to overlay maps of different themes, analyze the relations revealed, and compute spatial relationships; as a structured set of spatial information, the GIS has become a tool for automating geographical analysis and synthesis both an integrated software package for handling, processing, and analyzing geographical data and a computer database in which every item of information is tied to a precise geographic location
  • Raster approach- that tie involves dividing the study area into a set of rectangular cells and describing the content of each cell
  • Vector Approach- the precise location of each object-point, line, or area- in a distribution is described

- A GIS incorporates three primary components- data storage capability, computer graphics programs, and statistical packages

- Mental Maps- images about an area or an environment developed by an individual on the basis of information or impressions received, interpreted, and stored; we use this map in organizing our daily activities

- Spatial systems, Spatial distributions -the arrangement of a phenomenon across the Earth's surface and a graphical display of such an arrangement is an important tool in geographical and environmental statistics.

- Map legend- Maps give information by using symbols. Symbols can be figures, shapes, lines, and colors that show where places and things are on a map. A map's legend tells you what the symbols mean.

- the systems of geographic concern are those in which the functionally important variables are spatial- location, distance, direction, density, and other basic concepts; the systems they define are not the same as regions, although spatial systems may be the basis for regional identification.

- systems have components, and the analysis of the role of components help reveal the operation of the system as a whole; to conduct that analysis, individual system elements must be isolated for separate identification and manipulated to see their function within the structure of the system or subsystem; maps (can segregate at a level of generalization those system elements; they compress, simplify, and abstract reality; are a type of models) and models (simplified abstraction of reality, designed to clarify relationships between its elements; used to simplify complex situations, eliminate unimportant details, and isolate for special study and analysis the role of one or more elements) are the devices geographers use to achieve that isolation and separate study



Chapter 2: Roots And Meaning Of Culture

In later weeks, we will explore religion, language, and other aspects of human cultures more in-depth.  This week, please focus on this short chapter about humans and the spread of their culture by exploring possibilism and environmental determinism- two differing ways of looking at how we humans interact with our environment.

Read Chapter 2 “Roots and Meaning of Culture” in Fellmann textbook. Learn the components of culture and the nature of human-environment interactions. We will discuss culture hearths and origins and what the structure of culture is and the forms of cultural change.

- Geography- the study of the physical and cultural components of the world.

- Physical geography- pertains to the natural forces and landscapes of the world

- Cultural geography/human geography- studies the human characteristics of a location and how these in turn relate and are affected by the natural world; study of the many cultural aspects found throughout the world and how they relate to the spaces and places where they originate and then travel as people continually move across various areas.

- Traditional Cultural Geography- rooted in the belief that societies and cultures grow out of a landscape and also reshape the landscape.  Those following this school of thought focus mainly on the physical cultural world, such as buildings, parks, and architecture, in non-modern and rural communities.

- New Cultural Geography- came about in the 1980’s and focuses on contemporary and urban cultures, examining non-physical elements such as gender, language, and identity.

-"More-than-Representational" Geography- focuses on understanding a cultural landscape by physically witnessing the practices, focusing on how the human cultures are actually performed.

- Globalization- cultural integration that occurs as culture in the form of physical and nonphysical ideas and practices is exchanged throughout the world.  This can occur in four forms: Trade and transaction, Capital and investment movements, Migration of people, The spread of knowledge and ideas. This is interesting because as culture created in one space and environment moves to new places, it adapts and integrates alongside the area's native culture.

- 4 Traditions of Human Geography: Spatial Tradition (also called Locational Tradition), Area Studies Tradition (also called Regional Tradition), Man-Land Tradition (also called Human-Environmental, Human-Land, or Culture-Environment Tradition), and Earth Science Tradition

 

- Culture- the specialized behavior patterns, understandings, adaptations, and social systems that summarize a group of people’s learned way of life; an ever-present part of the regional differences that are the essence of human geography; the visible and invisible/tangible and intangible evidences of culture are all parts of the spatial diversity geographers study; cultural differences over time and area result in contrasts between Stone Age humans and urban Americans, and rural Zimbabwe and the US or subtle differences between Paris, Moscow, and New York; separate human groups developed techniques

to solve regionally varied problems, creating areally distinctive customs and ways of life

- culture is learned, not biological (instincts or genes)- transmitted within a society to generations by imitation, instruction, and example; as members of a social group, individuals acquire integrated sets of behavioral patterns, environmental and social perceptions, and knowledge of existing technologies; age, sex, status, and occupation dictate the aspects of the cultural whole in which one becomes indoctrinated

- Social structure- framework of roles and interrelationships of individuals and established groups

- Culture is a complexly interlocked web of behaviors and attitudes- its full and diverse content cannot be appreciated and may be wholly misunderstood if we concentrate on limited, obvious traits; they are fundamental cultural variables that give structure and spatial order to societies

- Culture traits- units of learned behavior ranging from objects, techniques, beliefs, and attitudes- these are the building blocks of the complex behavioral patterns of distinctive groups of people

- Culture complex- individual cultural traits that are functionally interrelated

- Cultural system- broader generalization than a cultural complex and refers to the collection of interacting cultural traits and cultural complexes that are shared by a group within a particular territory

- Cultural traits, complexes, and systems have areal extent- geographers are interested in their spatial distribution but more with…

- Culture region- portion of the earth’s surface occupied by populations sharing recognizable and distinctive cultural characteristics

- Culture realm- set of culture regions showing related culture complexes and landscapes- culture regions at the broadest scale of recognition- may mislead more than they inform- validity is questioned in light of globalization of all aspects of society and economy

- Globalization is a homogenization of cultures as economics are integrated and uniform consumer demands satisfied- cultural flows, exchanges, and increasing mobility of people, goods, and information has reduced the compartmentalized ethnicities, languages, and religions- worldwide intermixture of peoples and customs; however, rather than leveling and removing regional contrasts, globalization is still countered by forces of regionalism, place identity, and ethnicity

- Cultural ecology- the study of the relationship between a culture group and the natural environment it occupies- interrelations of people to the environment of a given area, their perceptions and utilization of it, and their impact on it

- Environmental determinism- the physical environment exclusively shapes humans., their actions, and their thoughts

- Possibilism- viewpoints promoted by late 19-20th century French geographer Paul Vidal de la Blache that people, not environments, are the dynamic forces of cultural development; the needs, traditions, and levels of technology of a culture affect how that culture assesses the possibilities of an area and shape what choices the culture makes regarding them; societies use natural resources in accordance with their circumstances; changes in a group’s technical abilities or objectives bring about changes in its perceptions of the usefulness of the land- basically, the impact of the environment is inversely related to the level of development of a culture while perception of environmental opportunities increase directly with growth in economic and cultural development

- The more technologically advanced and complex the culture, the more apparent is its impact on the natural landscape; urban-industrial societies- cultural landscape has come to outweigh the natural physical environment in its impact on daily lives- interposes itself between nature and humans

- Cultural landscape- the earth’s surface as modified by human action- tangible physical record of a given culture

- Paleolithic- Old Stone Age- the period at the end of glaciation during which small and scattered groups like the ivory hunters began to develop regional variations in their ways of life and livelihood

- Animal, plant, and human populations had been spatially confined by both the ice margin and the harsh climates of middle-latitude regions but began to spread, colonizing newly opened territories; Human migration increased population; improved tool technology greatly extended the range of possibilities in the use of locally available materials; the result was a more efficient and extensive exploitation of the physical environment than earlier had been possible; regional contrasts in plant and animal life and in environmental conditions also accelerated the differentiation of culture between isolated groups who had shared common characteristics

- Hunter-gatherers- pre-agricultural people dependent on the year-round availability of plant and animal foodstuffs they could secure with the rudimentary stone tools and weapons at their disposal

- By the end of the Ice Age, language, religion, long-distance trade, permanent settlements, and social stratification within groups appear to have been well developed in many European culture areas; what was learned and created was transmitted within the cultural group- the increasing variety of adaptive strategies and technologies and the diversity of noneconomic creations meant an inevitable cultural variation of humankind

- Mesolithic- Middle Stone Age- 11,000 to 5000 BC- transition from the collection of food to its production

- Beginning of periods of cultural evolution leading from hunting and gathering economies through agriculture and animal husbandry to urbanization and industrialization

- The stages of the Stone Age occurring during different time spans in different world areas mark distinctive changes in tools, tasks, and social complexities of cultures

- As population rose and climatic fluctuation affected established plant and food sources, people began domestication of plants and animals; while adapting wild plants to agricultural purposes, the human cultivators adapted too- sedentary residence to protect planted areas, labor specializations, more formalized and expansive religious structures with fertility and harvest rites; the regional contrasts between hunter-gather and sedentary agricultural societies increased

- Cultural divergence

- Carrying capacity- number of persons supportable within a given area by the technologies at their disposal

- Neolithic- New Stone Age- stage of cultural development- creation of advanced set of tools and technologies to deal with conditions and needs encountered by an expanding, sedentary population whose economy was based on the agricultural management of the environment- approx. 8000 to 3500 BC- brought revolutionary ad complex changes to human life

- On the foundation of specialized technical advancements, a complex exploitative culture appeared and a formal economy emerged- stratified society based on labor and role specialization replaced rough equality of adults in hunting and gathering economies; special local advantages in resources or products promoted development of long-distance trading connections

- By the end of the Neolithic period: spatially restricted groups undertook the purposeful restructuring of their environment, new and more formalized rules of conduct and control emerged (punishment, law, private property), enforcement of rules of societies were increasingly stratified by social privileges and economic status, religions became more formalized, and occupations became specialized

- the social and technical revolutions of the Neolithic period were spatially confined; new technologies, ways of life, and social structures diffused from those points of origin and were selectively adopted by people not a party of their creation

- Culture hearth- centers of innovation and invention from which key culture traits and elements moved to exert influence on surrounding regions- cradle of any culture group whose developed systems of livelihood and life created a distinctive cultural landscape; complex social, intellectual, and technological bases that served as cultural diffusion centers

- Civilizations- indicators are writing, metallurgy, long-distance trade connections, astronomy and math, social stratification, and labor specialization- mostly urban centered

- Each hearth showed a rigorous organization of agriculture resulting in local productivity sufficient to enable a significant number of people to engage in nonfarm activities; creation of stratified societies; development of astronomy, math, and calendar; exporting of technologies, skills, and learned behaviors

- Multilinear evolution- proposed by Julian Steward (1902-1972); to explain common characteristics of widely separated cultures developed under similar ecological circumstances- each major environmental zone-arid, high altitude, midlatitude steppe, tropical forest- tends to induce common adaptive traits in the cultures of those who exploit it; those traits were founded on the development of agriculture and the emergence of similar cultural and administrative structures in several culture hearths; but similar is not identical- since comparable sequences of developmental events cannot always or even often be explained on the basis of borrowing or exporting of ideas and techniques (because of time and space differences), they must be regarded as evidence of parallel creations based on similar ecologies; from similar origins but through separate adaptations and innovations, distinctive cultures emerged

- Diffusionism- belief that cultural similarities occur primarily by spatial spread (diffusion) from one or a few common origin sites; cultural advancement and civilizations are passed on along trade routes and through group contact rather than being the result of separate and independent creation

  • Artifacts- material objects to fill our basic needs
  • Sociological subsystem- sum of those expected and accepted patterns of interpersonal relations that find their outlet in economic, political, military, religious, kinship, and other associations
  • Sociofacts- define the social organization of a culture; regulate how individuals function in a group

- All innovation has a radiating impact on the web of culture; the more basic the innovation, the more pervasive its consequences; the rate of invention has increased and the period between idea conception and product availability has decreased- the more ideas available and the more minds to exploit and combine them, the greater the rate of innovation- larger urban centers of advanced technologies tend to be centers of innovation because of their size and number of ideas interchanged; ideas not only stimulate new thoughts and viewpoints but also create circumstances in which the society must develop new solutions to maintain its forward momentum

  • Diffusion- process by which an idea or innovation is transmitted from one individual or group to another across space- two processes:

- Relocation diffusion: people move to a new area and take their cultures with them- innovation or idea is physically carried and transported to new areas by carriers who permanently leave their home locale- migrating individuals or populations

- Expansion diffusion: spread of an item or idea from one place to another, with the thing diffused frequently intensified in the origin area; information about an innovation may spread through a society perhaps aided by local or mass media advertising, or new adopters of an ideology or way of life may be inspired or recruited by immigrant or native converts- a phenomenon spreads from one place to neighboring locations, but in the process remains and is often intensified in the place of origin

         -  Contagious diffusion- when expansion diffusion affects uniformly all individuals and areas outward from the source region- importance of direct contact between those who developed or have adopted the innovation and those who newly encounter it

- If an idea has merit and people become adopters, the number of contacts of adopters with potential adopters will compound, so innovation will spread slowly at first and then more and more rapidly until saturation occurs or a barrier is reached; the incidence of adoption under contagious diffusion is represented by the S-curve

- Time-distance decay- can affect the rate of diffusion of a trait or idea - spread or acceptance of an idea is usually delayed as distance from the source of the innovation increases

- In some instances geographic distance is less important in the transfer of ideas than communication between major centers or important people

          - Hierarchical diffusion- process of transferring ideas first between larger places or prominent people and later to smaller or less important points or people

- Space-time compression- while the diffusion of ideas can be slowed by time-distance decay, their speed of spread can be increased to the point of becoming instantaneous by this concept, made possible by modern communication- modern communication technology has encouraged and facilitated globalization of culture

           - Stimulus diffusion- a fundamental idea, though not the specific trait itself, stimulates imitative behavior within a receptive population

- In both expansion and relocation diffusion, innovations in technological or ideological subsystems are readily diffused to cultures that have basic similarities and compatibilities; diffusion cannot be viewed solely as the outcome of knowledge dispersed the acceptance of new traits, articles, or ways of doing or thinking by a potential receiving population depends not just on information flow to that population but also to entire cultural and economic structure

- Culture is a complex organized system and culture change involves alteration of the system’s established structure in ways that may be rejected even after knowledge is received

- Independent/parallel invention- cultural similarities do not necessarily prove that diffusion has occurred

  • Acculturation- a culture group may undergo major modifications in its own identifying traits by adopting some or all of the characteristics of another dominant culture group- where immigrant populations take on the values, attitudes, customs, and speech of the receiving society, which itself undergoes change from absorption of the arriving group

- Contact and subsequent cultural alteration may occur in a conquered or colonized region where the subordinate or subject population is forced to adopt the culture, introduced through relocation diffusion, or done voluntarily as a result of being overwhelmed by the superiority in numbers or the technical level of the conqueror

- Primitive indigenous groups brought into contact with conquering or absorbing societies may cease to exist as separate cultural entities; however, close contact between two different groups may involve adjustments of the original cultural patterns of both instead of the disappearance of either

          - Transculturation- where the two-way flow reflects a more equal exchange of cultural outlooks and ways of life

- All cultures are amalgams of innumerable innovations spread spatially from their points of origin and integrated into the structure of the receiving societies

- Since the pace of innovation is affected strongly by mixing of ideas among alert, responsive people and is increased by exposure to a variety of cultures, the most active and innovative historical hearths of culture were those at crossroads locations and those deeply involved in distant trade and colonization

- Recent changes in technology permit us to travel farther than ever before, with greater safety and speed, and to communicate without physical contact more easily and completely than was previously possible; this intensification of contact has resulted in the acceleration of innovation and the rapid spread of goods and ideas

- Diffusion barriers- any obstacles and conditions that hinder either the flow of information or the movement of people and thus retard or prevent the acceptance of an innovation

- Absorbing barrier- halts spread of an innovation- friction of distance/time-distance decay

- Interrupting barriers- interregional contact can also be hindered by physical environment and lack of receptivity- delaying or deflecting the path of diffusion- cultural barriers (political restrictions, religion)

- Permeable barriers- permit passage and acceptance of at least some innovations encountering them; the more similar two cultural areas are, the greater the likelihood of the adoption of an innovation- diffusion is a selective process- the decision to adopt is governed by the receiving group’s culture

- Syncretism- fusion of old and new, major feature of culture change; the alteration of meaning or form of diffused ideas and artifacts that makes them acceptable to a borrowing group- conformity (food)

Spatial interaction- the movement of peoples, ideas, and commodities within and between areas- designed to achieve effective integration between different points of human activity; it represents the attempt to smooth out the spatially differing availability of required resources, commodities, information, or opportunities

Commodity flows: responses to the differences of distribution of the world’s resources and products; links between points of supply and locales of demand

Edward Ullman- speculated on the essential conditions affecting interactions of commodity flows and proposed a model- spatial interaction is controlled by complementarity, transferability, and intervening opportunity

Complementarity- for two places to interact, one place must have a supply for which there is an effective demand in the other

Transferability- acceptable costs of exchange; expression of the mobility of a commodity and a function of three interrelated conditions: characteristics and value of the product, distance measured in time and money penalties, and the ability of the commodity to bear the costs of movement

Intervening Opportunity- more attractive alternative sources of supply or demand closer at hand or cheaper

We focus on general principles that govern frequency and intensity of interaction both to validate the three preconditions of spatial exchange and to establish the probability that any given potential interaction will occur

Friction of distance- distance has a retarding effect on human interaction

Distance decay- interchange decreases as distance increases; decline of an activity or function with increasing distance from its point of origin

Inverse square relationship- interaction between places is inversely related to the square of the distance separating them

Gravity model- taken from Henry Carey, who observed that Newton’s law of universal gravitation was applicable to the aggregate actions of humans; big things have a stronger attraction force (gravitational pull) than small objects and things close to each other have  stronger mutual attraction than objects at greater distance

The gravity model assures us that although spatial interaction always tends to decrease with increasing distance, at a certain point it tends to expand with increases

Carey had two interests: interaction between urban centers (solved by converting physical mass in the gravity model to population size while retaining the distance component of Newtonian calculation) and the fact that large cities have greater drawing power for individuals than do small ones (addressed by law of retail gravitation/Reilly’s law: Reilly determined the relative amount of retail trade that two cities would attract from an intermediate place in the vicinity of the breaking point, using the population and distance inputs of the gravity model; Reilly’s law stated that two cities will attract trade from intermediate locales in direct proportion to the populations of the two cities and in inverse proportion to the square of the distance of these two cities to the intermediate place; it provides a quick determination of the trade boundary/breaking point of two places; this will define the “trade area”

THe gravity model can be used to account for a wide variety of flow patterns; each pattern suggests that size as well as distance influence spatial interaction and help us understand friction distance

-Von Thunen Model- Early in the 19th century, Johann Heinrich von Thunen observed that lands of apparently identical physical properties were utilized for different agricultural purposes. Around each major urban market center, he noted, there developed a set of concentric land use rings of different farm products. The ring closest to the market specialized in perishable commodities that were both expensive to ship and in high demand. The high prices they could command in the urban market made their production an appropriate use of high-valued land near the city. Surrounding rings of farmlands farther away from the city were used for less perishable commodities with lower transport costs, reduced demand, and lower market prices. General farming and grain farming replaced the market gardening of the inner ring. At the outer margins of profitable agriculture, farthest from the single central market, livestock grazing and similar extensive land uses were found.

 

To explain why this should be so, von Thunen constructed a formal spatial model-perhaps the first developed to analyze human activity patterns. He concluded that the uses to which parcels were put was a function of the differing rent values placed on seemingly identical lands. Those differences reflected the cost of overcoming the distance separating a given farm from a central market town. The greater the distance, the higher was the operating cost to the farmer, since transport charges had to be added to other expenses. A simple exchange relationship ensued: the greater the transportation costs, the lower the rent that could be paid for land if the crop produced was to remain competitive in the market

The theoretical relationship between supply, demand, and price is simple. If demand for a good or services exceeds its available supply, scarcity will drive up the price it can command in the marketplace. That is, the higher the price of a good, the more of it will be offered in the market. When the price is very high, however, relatively few people are inclined to buy. To dispose of their increased output, old and new producers of the commodity are forced to reduce prices to enlarge the market by making the good affordable to a larger number of potential customers. That is, at lower prices, more of a good will be purchased. If the price falls too low, production or sale becomes unprofitable and inefficient suppliers are forced out of business, reducing supply.

-Line-Haul (Over-The-Road) Costs- Vary with the individual shipments and are the expenses involved in the actual movement of commodities once they have been loaded.

-Tapering Principle- The result of when carrier costs have a tendency to decline as the length of haul increases because scale economies in long-haul movement permit the averaging of total costs over a greater number of miles. The actual costs of transport, including terminal charges and line costs, increase at a decreasing rate as fixed costs are spread over longer hauls. The tapering of company cost is differently expressed among media because their mixes of fixed and variable costs are different.

-Break-Of-Bulk Points- sites where goods have to be transferred or transshipped from one carrier to another.

-In-Transit Privilege- As a traffic-generating inducement, may be granted to a manufacturer by a transportation agency through the quotation of a special single rate from material source to market for a movement that may be interrupted for processing or manufacturing en route.

 

-Industrial Location Theories-

-Least-Cost Theory- Based on the work of Alfred Weber (1868-1958) and sometimes called Weberian analysis. It explains the optimum location of a manufacturing establishment based on minimizing three basic expenses: relative transport costs, labor costs, and agglomeration (the clustering of productive activities and people for mutual advantage) costs. Weber concluded that transport costs are the major consideration determining location. He made five controlling assumptions: (1) An area is completely uniform physically, politically, culturally, and technologically. Known as the uniform, or isotropic, plain assumption. (2) Manufacturing involves a single product to be shipped to a single market whose location is known. (3) Inputs involve raw materials from more than one known source location. (4) Labor is infinitely available but immobile in location. (5) Transportation routes are not fixed but connect origin and destination by the shortest path; and transport costs directly reflect the weight of items shipped and the distance they are moved. Given these assumptions, Weber derived the least transport cost location by means of the locational triangle. It diagrams the cost consequences of fixed locations of materials and market and of movement in any direction of a given weight of commodity at a uniform cost per unit of distance. The problem is to locate the optimum point of production where the total ton-distance involved in assembling materials and distributing the product is at a minimum. Each corner of the triangle exerts its pull; each has a defined cost of production should it be chosen as the plant site. Weberian analysis aims at the least transport cost location, which most likely will be an intermediate point somewhere within the locational triangle. Its exact position will depend on distances, the respective weights of the raw material inputs, and the final weight of the finished product, and may be either material (reflecting a sizable weight loss during the production process) or market (indicating a weight gain) oriented.

 

-Locational Interdependence Theory- Exists when the locational decision of one firm is influenced by locations chosen by its competitors. It influences the manner in which competitive firms with identical cost structures arrange themselves in space to assure themselves a measure of spatial monopoly in their  combined market. In locational interdependence theory, the concern is with variable revenue analysis rather than, as in the Weber model, with variable costs. The simplest case concerns the locational decisions of two firms in competition with each other to supply identical goods to customers evenly spaced along a linear market. The usual example is of two ice cream vendors, each selling the same brand at the same price along a stretch of beach having a uniform distribution of people. All will purchase the same amount of ice cream (that is, demand is inelastic- is not sensitive to a change in the price) and will patronize the seller nearer to them. Suggestion is that the two sellers would eventually cluster in the mid-point of the linear market so that each vendor could supply customers at the extremities of the market without yielding locational advantage to the single competitor. This is a spatial solution that maximizes return but does not minimize costs. The lowest total cost location for each of the two vendors would be at the midpoint of his or her half of the beach. To maximize market share, however, one seller might decide to relocate immediately next to the competitor, dominating now three-fourths of the entire market. The logical retaliation would be for the second seller to jump back over the first to recapture market share. Ultimately, side by side location at the centerline of the beach is inevitable and a stable placement is achieved since neither seller can gain any further advantage from moving. The economist Harold Hotelling (1895-1973), who is usually associated with the locational interdependence approach, expanded the conclusion about clustered ice cream sellers to a more generalized statement explaining industrial concentration by multiple producers under conditions of identical production costs and inelastic market demand. However, if the market becomes sensitive to price, sales to more distance customers will be discouraged and producers seeking to maximize sales will again separate rather than aggregate. The conclusion then is that price sensitivity (elasticity of demand) will encourage industrial dispersion.

 

-Profit-Maximization Approaches- For many theorists, the simplicities and rigidities of the least-cost and the locational interdependence explanations are unrealistically restrictive. Ultimately, they maintain, the correct location of fa production facility is where the net profit is greatest. They propose employing a substitution principle that recognizes that in many industrial processes it is possible to replace a declining amount of one input with an increase in another, or to increase transportation costs while simultaneously reducing land rent. With substitution, a number of different points may be appropriate manufacturing locations. Further, they suggest, a whole series of points may exist where total revenue of an enterprise just equals its total cost of producing a given output. These points, connected, mark the spatial margin of profitability and define the larger area within which profitable operation is possible. Location anywhere within the margin assures some profit and tolerates both imperfect knowledge and personal (rather than economic) considerations. Such less-than-optimal, but still acceptable, sites are considered satisficing locations. For some firm, spatial margins may be very broad because transport costs area negligible factor in production and marketing. Such firms are said to be footloose, that is, neither resource nor market oriented.

 

-Fordism- Assembly line production of identical commodities by a rigidly controlled and specialized labor force for generalized mass markets-known as Fordism to recognize Henry Ford’s pioneering development of the system-became less realistic in both market and technology terms.

-Flexible Manufacturing- Processes based on smaller production runs of a greater variety of goods aimed at smaller, niche markets than were catered to by traditional manufacturing have become common.

-Agglomeration- The spatial concentration of people and activities for mutual benefit.

-Agglomeration (External) Economies- Benefits for individual firms that they could not experience in isolation.

-Infrastructure- Shared transport facilities, social services, communication facilities, public utilities, and other installations and services needed to facilitate industrial and other forms of economic development.

-Multiplier Effect- Causes each new firm added to the agglomeration to lead to the further development of infrastructure and linkages.

-Deglomeration- A disadvantage of agglomeration. When the costs of aggregation exceed the benefits, a firm will actually profit by relocating to a more isolated position.

-Just-In-Time (JIT) Manufacturing- In contrast to deglomeration, seeks to reduce inventories for the production process by purchasing inputs for arrival just in time to use and producing output just in time to sell.

-Flexible Production Systems- JIT is one expression of a transition from mass-production Fordism to more flexible production systems. That flexibility is designed to allow producers to shift quickly and easily between different levels of output and important to move from one factory process or product to another as market demand dictates.

 

-Comparative Advantage- Tells us that areas and countries can best improve their economies and living standards through specialization and trade. These benefits will follow if each area or country concentrates on the production of those items for which it has the greatest relative advantage over other areas ofr for which it has the least relative disadvantage and imports all other goods.

-Outsourcing- Producing parts or products abroad for domestic sale. Has also come to mean subcontracting production and service sector work to outside, often nonunion, domestic companies.

-Offshoring- The practice of either hiring foreign workers or, commonly, contracting with a foreign third-party service provider to take over and run particular business processes or operations, such as call centers or accounting, billing, and similar nonproduction “back office” aspects of manufacturing.

-Hypercompetition- Traditional notions of comparative advantage are disappearing in the face of a new era of hypercompetition, at least in business and professional services.

-New International Division Of Labor (NIDL)- The exploitation of comparative advantage and utilization of outsourcing and offshoring, by transferring technology from economically advanced to underdeveloped economies is transforming the world economy by introducing NIDL. Builds on the current trend toward the increased subdivision of manufacturing processing into smaller steps (and a similar fragmentation of stages of professional services).

-Transnational Corporations (TNCs)- private firms that have established branch operations in nations foreign to their headquarters’ country.

-Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)- the purchase or construction of factories and other fixed assets by TNCs- has been a significant engine of globalization.

-Export Processing Zones (EPCs)- Concentrates much of new plant investment and expanded developing country industrial production. Recently created within those countries. May be either a delimited geographical area or, frequently, an export-oriented manufacturing enterprise located anywhere within a host country that benefits from special investment incentives.

-Megalopolis/Conurbation- An extended urbanized area formed by the gradual merger of several individual cities.

-Gross Value Added (GVA)- Linked to GDP; the link can get defined as GVA + taxes on products - subsidies on products = GDP.

 

-Primary Activities- Gather, extract, or grow things.

-Secondary Activities- Give form utility to the products of primary industry through manufacturing and processing efforts.

-Tertiary Activities- A major and growing segment of both domestic and international economic activity that involves services rather than the production of commodities. Consist of business and labor specializations that provide services to the primary and secondary sectors, to the general community, and to the individual. They imply pursuits other than the actual production of tangible commodities. Tertiary services fulfill the exchange function of advanced economies and provide the market transactions necessary in highly interdependent societies. In commercial activities, they also provide vitally needed information about market demand, without which economically justifiable production decisions are impossible.

-Effective Demand- The supply of low-level services of necessity must be identical to the spatial distribution of effective demand, that is, wants made meaningful through purchasing power.

-Tourism- Travel undertaken for purposes of recreation rather than business. Has become not only the most important single tertiary sector activity but is, as well, the world’s largest industry in jobs and total value generated.

-Quaternary Activities- May be seen realistically as an advanced form of services involving specialized knowledge, technical skills, communication ability, or administrative competence.

-Quinary Activities- The gold collar professions of the chapter title, another separately recognized subdivision of the tertiary sector representing the special and highly paid skills of top business executives, government officials, and the like. These people find their place of business in major metropolitan centers, in and near major universities and research parks, at first-rank medical centers, and in cabinet and department-level offices of political capitals.

 

-Postindustrial- The essence of the transition steadily away from production and processing and toward the trade, personal, and professional services of the tertiary sector and the information and control activities of the quaternary and quinary.

- Development- the extent to which the human and natural resources of an area or country have been brought into full productive use

- Underdevelopment- the possibility or desirability of applying additional capital, labor, or technology to the resource base of an area to permit the present population to improve its material well being or to allow populations to increase without a deterioration in their quality of life

- Core periphery model- based on the observation that within many spatial systems sharp territorial contrasts exist in wealth, economic advancement, and growth- in development- between economic heartlands and outlying subordinate zones

- Circular and cumulative causation- growth in the developing core must come at the expense of the peripheries of the country; this continues to polarize development and leads to a permanent division between prosperous (dominating) cores and depressed (exploited) peripheral districts milked of surplus labor, raw materials, and profits.

-Trickle-down effects/Spread effects- income convergence will occur this way, working to diffuse benefits outward from the center in the form of higher prices paid for needed materials or through the dispersion of technology to branch plants or contract suppliers in lower-cost regions of production. -Gross domestic product (GDP)- total market value of all final goods and services produced annually within the borders of a country.

-Gross national income (GNI)- Adds to GDP the total foreign income earned by its citizens (gross national income was formerly known by gross national product).

-Purchasing power parity (PPP)- Often applied to GNI to account for price differences.

 

-Three adjustments to GNI to compare development-

  1. Convert each country’s currency into a single measure, typically U.S. dollar equivalents.
  2. Divide by the number of people in a country to get GNI per capita.
  3. Note that market rates of currency exchange fluctuate with business cycles and trading patterns and do not necessarily correspond to prices in a particular country.

 

-Informal Economy- shadow, gray, underground economy; In all countries, at least a portion of goods and services is produced and workers supported by informal economy. In developing countries as a group, the informal economy (2002) is equivalent to 41% of their official gross domestic product.

-Gross global product- the total value of goods and services produced by the world economy.

-Technology- the totality of tools and methods available to and used by a culture group in producing items essential to its subsistence and comfort.

-Technology gap- A contrast in the range and productivity of artifacts introduced at the core and those known or employed at the periphery.

-Cultural convergence- The increasing similarity in technologies and ways of life among societies at the same levels of development.

-Technology transfer- Placing in one’s own territory and under one’s own control the productive plants and processes marking the more advanced countries.

 

Primary economic measures commonly accepted as most revealing of the relative progress of countries of the South along the scale of development:

  • Gross national income and purchasing power parity per capita
  • Per capita energy consumption
  • Percent of the workforce engaged in agriculture
  • Calorie intake per capita /0p;

-Per capita energy consumption- a common measure of technological advancement of nations because it loosely correlates with per capita income, degree of industrialization, and use of advanced technology.

-Landlessness- a function of an imbalance between the size of the agricultural labor force and the arable land resource. Frequently a reflection of concentration of ownership by a few and consequent landlessness for many.

 

-Rostow Five Successive Stages of Growth and Advancement-

  1. Traditional societies: poorly developed.
  2. Preconditions for takeoff established.
  3. Takeoff: critical developmental stage, lasting perhaps 20-30 years, during which rates of investment increase, new industries are established, resources are exploited, and growth becomes the expected norm.

-Gender- in a cultural sense, refers to socially created, not biologically based, distinctions between femininity and masculinity.

This is one of my very favorite parts of AP Human Geography - cities!  Urban concepts are heavily tested in the AP Human Geography exam so pay close attention to the following weeks of new information on cities!  First we will talk about the origins of the concept of "city"!  Then, we will learn about the role they play in today's world both locally and on an international scale.  Europeans cities and non-Western cities are examined and we look at the ways space is organized within a city.  

Read Chapter 11 “Urban Systems and Urban Structures” in Fellmann textbook. We will learn about the nature of cities – origins, definitions, and locations – in the world. Also, we will cover the economic base and systems of cities: functions, hierarchies, and networks. Urban land use, social areas, and patterns of change in urban areas will be discussed. World urban diversity – Western and non-Western cities will be compared and contrasted.

 

-Megacities- Metropolises with populations of 10 million or more.

-Conurbations- Extensive metropolitan regions created when separate major metropolitan complexes of whatever size expand along the superior transportation facilities connecting them, meet, and bind together at their outer margins. Where this increasingly common pattern has emerged, the urban landscape can no longer be described in simple terms.

-Megalopolis- The original and largest megalopolis that contains the economic, political, and administrative core of the US: the nearly continuous urban string that stretches from north of Boston to south of Washington D.C.

-Cities- Among the oldest markets of civilization.  Though flawed, remain the capstone of our cultures, the organizing focuses of modern societies and economies, and the magnet of people everywhere.

-Hinterland- The productive area surrounding a population center. Cities depended on having close reciprocal relationships with these.

-Tell- An occupation mound.



-The Nature Of Cities- All cities show regularities appropriate to their time and place of existence:

  1. All perform functions-have an economic base-generating the income necessary to support themselves and their contained population.
  2. None exists in a vacuum; each is part of a larger society and economy with which it has essential reciprocal connections. Each is a unit in a system of cities and a focus for a surrounding non urban area.
  3. Has a more or less orderly internal arrangement of land uses, social groups, and economic functions.

 

-Site- The exact terrain features associated with the city, as well as-less usefully-to its absolute (Globe Grid) location.

-Special Placement Circumstances- These include break-of-bulk locations such as river crossing points where cargoes and people must interrupt a journey, head-of-navigation (bay head) locations where the limits of water transportation are reached, and railhead locations where a railroad ended.

 

-Economic Base- From their ancient beginnings, cities depended on close reciprocal relationships with their hinterlands. They provided the market where rural produce could be exchanged for the goods produced and the defense or religious functions performed by the city. Such rural service functions remain important. However, not all of the activities carried on within a city are intended to  connect that city with the outside world. Some are necessary simply to support the city itself. Together, these two levels of activity make up the economic base of an urban settlement. Understanding the growth or decline of cities hinges on grasping the relationship between the two sectors.

-Basic Sector- Engagement either in the production of goods or the performance of services for areas and people outside the city itself.

-Nonbasic Sector- Producing goods or services for residents of the urban unit itself.

-Multiplier Effect- Means that a city’s employment and population grow with the addition of non basic workers and dependents as a supplement to new basic employment. The size is determined by the community’s basic/nonbasic ratio.

-Transportation Centers- The spatial pattern of transportation centers is that of alignment. Routes of communication form the orienting axes along which cities developed and on which at least their initial functional success depended.

-Special-Function Cities- Those engaged in mining, manufacturing, or other activities the localization of which is related to raw material occurrence, agglomeration economies, or the circular and cumulative attractions of constantly growing market and labor concentrations.

 

-Central Places- Nodes for the distribution of economic goods and services to surrounding non urban populations.

-Central Place Theory- Developed by the geographer Walter Christaller. To explain those observed settlement size and spacing regularities. He observed a pattern of interdependent small, medium, and larger towns that together could provide the goods and services needed by a dispersed rural population. Small towns would serve as marketplaces for frequently required low order commodities and services, while expensive luxury goods would be available only in larger communities that were central to a number of surrounding small towns. People, that is, would have to travel only short distances for basic things, and longer distances for expensive and infrequently demanded goods and services. This explanation and description of the urban size and spacing regularities he observed have been shown to be generally applicable in widely differing regions of the world. When varying incomes, cultures, physical landscapes, and transportation systems are taken into consideration, his theory holds up rather well.

 

-Urban Influence Zones- The areas outside of a city that are still affected by it.

-The Urban Hierarchy- Probably the most effective way to recognize how systems of cities are organized. A ranking of cities based on their size and functional complexity.

-World Cities- Standing at the top of national systems of cities are a relatively few agglomerations that may be called world cities. These large urban centers are control points for international production, marketing, and finance.

-Rank-Size Rule- Summarizes the city size hierarchy in some countries, especially those with complex economies and a long urban history. It tells us that the nth largest city of a national system of cities will be 1/n the size of the largest city. That is, the second largest settlement will be ½ the size of the largest, the 10th biggest will be 1/10 the size of the first ranked city. Although no national city system exactly meets the requirements of the rank-size rule, that of Russia and the US closely approximates it. Less applicable to countries with developing economies or where the city system is dominated by a primate city.

-Primate City- a city that is far more than twice the size of the second ranked city.

-Network City- In recent years a new kind of urban spatial pattern has begun to appear, one seemingly divorced from the more recognized hierarchical orderings of cities we have reviewed. Evolves when two or more previously independent nearby cities, potentially complementary in functions, strive to cooperate by developing between them high-speed transportation corridors and communications infrastructure.

-Urban- Often used to describe such places as a town, city, suburb, and metropolitan area, but it is a general term, not used to specify a particular type r size of settlement. Not everyone uses terms designating the different types of urban settlement in the same way.

-City/Town- Denote nucleated settlements, multifunctional in character, including an established central business district and both residential and nonresidential land uses. Towns are smaller in size and have less functional complexity than cities, but they still have a nuclear business concentration.

-Suburb- Implies a subsidiary area, a functionally specialized segment of a larger urban complex. It may be dominantly or exclusively residential, industrial, or commercial, but by the specialization of its land uses and functions, a suburb is not self-sufficient. However, can be independent political entities.

-Central City- The principal core of a larger urban area, separately incorporated and ringed by its dependent suburbs.

-Urbanized Area- A continuously built-up landscape defined by building and population densities with no reference to political boundaries. May be viewed as the physical city and may contain a central city and many contiguous cities, towns, suburbs, and other urban tracts.

-Metropolitan Area- Contrasting from urbanized area, refers to a large-scale functional entity, perhaps containing several urbanized areas, discontinuously built-up but nonetheless operating as an integrated economic whole.

-Metropolitan Statistical Areas- Comprised of a central county or counties with at least one urbanized area of at least 50,000 population, plus adjacent outlying counties with a high degree of social and economic integration with the central county as measured by commuting volumes.

-Central Business District (CBD)- Within the older central city, the radiating mass transit lines focused on the original city center, giving that area the highest accessibility within the growing urban complex and, therefore, the greatest attraction for those functions profiting most from a central location. Building lots within the emerging CBD, as a result of their scarcity and accessibility, could command high rental and purchase prices.

-Hot Corner- Peak intersection of mass transit convergence.

 

-Concentric Zone Model- Developed by University of Chicago sociologists the explain the structuring of American cities in the 1920s. It describes the urban community as a set of nested rings of mostly residential diversity at increasing distances in all directions from the CBD fringe. The first, a zone of transition, is characterized by stagnation and deterioration and contains high-density, low-income slums, rooming houses, and perhaps ethnic ghettos. Next outward is a zone of workers’ homes, usually smaller older houses on small lots. The third zone houses better residences, single-family homes or higher-rent apartments for those able to exercise choice in housing location and afford the longer journey to CBD employment. Finally, just beginning to emerge when this model was proposed, was an outer zone of low-density suburban development. The concentric zone model is dynamic. Each type of land use and each residential group tends to move outward into the next outer zone as the city matures and expands. That movement was seen as part of a ceaseless process of invasion and succession that yielded a restructured land use pattern and population segregation by income level. The social patterning of Chicago shows some accordance with this model.

 

-Sector Model- Devised in the 1930s by the land economist Homer Hoyt. Focuses on transportation arterials. It posits that high-rent residential areas are dominant in city expansion and grow outward from the city center along major transportation routes such as streetcar and elevated railroad lines or suburban commuter routes. As cities grow, the model suggests, the highest-income groups move into new homes in new neighborhoods located along the extending transit lines radiating outward from the center of the city. Middle-income housing follows and clusters around the residences of the wealthy, and low-income populations occupy districts adjacent to the areas of industry and associated transportation, such as freight railroad lines. The sector model is also dynamic, marked by a filtering down process as older areas are abandoned by the outward movement of their original higher-income inhabitants, with the lowest-income populations (closest to the city center and furthest from the current location of the wealthy) becoming the inheritors of the least-desirable vacated area. The expansion of the city is radial.

 

-Multiple-Nuclei Model- Contrasts the sector model. Proposed by geographers Chauncy Harris and Edward Ullman. In the view, large cities developed by peripheral spread from several nodes of growth, not just one. Certain activities have specific locational requirements: the retail district needs accessibility; a port function needs a waterfront site; heavy industry requires level land adjacent to railroads. Peripheral expansion of the separate nuclei eventually leads to coalescence and the meeting of incompatible land uses along the lines of juncture. The urban land use pattern, therefore, is not regularly structured from a single center in a sequence of circles of a series of sectors but based on separately expanding clusters of contrasting activities. Gives a better insight into metropolitan area structuring of the more recent past and present, but should be supplemented by newer visualizations of contemporary metropolitan complexes or galactic cities.

 

-Peripheral Model- Takes into account the major changes in urban form that have taken place since WWII, especially the suburbanization of various central city functions. The peripheral model focuses on the peripheral belt that lies within the metropolitan area, but outside the central city itself. Functions of peripheral belt communities are defined not by their relationship to the metropolitan center, but to other parts of the peripheral zone. In these models, circumferential highways and expressways outside the central city make large tracts of land available for development in the low-density sprawl characteristic of individual rather than mass transit movement of people. Residences are segregated by price level into relatively homogenous suburban clusters, and individual nodes in the peripheral belt are centers for employment or services. Much of the life of the residents of the periphery takes place outside the central city.

 

-Social Status- Determined by income, education, occupation, and home value, though it may be measured differently in different cultures. Agrees with the sector model. Today, social status divisions are often perpetuated between separate municipalities, which may differ greatly in relative income. Many residential developments are also income-segregated because their houses are of similar value. To preserve the upscale nature of a development and protect land values, self-governing community associations may be formed to enact and enforce land use restrictions.

-Family Status- As the distance from the city center increases, the average age of the adult residents declines, or the sizes of their family increases, or both. Within a particular sector-say, that of high status-older people whose children do not live with them and young professionals without families tend to live close to the city center. In contrast, young families seeking space for child rearing locate farther from the city center, while singles and empty nesters may covet more the accessibility of the cultural and business life of the urban core. However, where inner-city life is unpleasant, there is a tendency for older people to migrate to the suburbs or to retirement communities. The arrangement that emerges is a concentric circle patterning according to family status.

-Ethnicity- For some groups, ethnicity is a more important residential location determinant than is social or family status. Areas of homogeneous ethnic identification appear in the social geography of cities as separate clusters or nuclei, reminiscent of the multiple-nuclei concept of urban structure. For some ethnic groups, cultural segregation is both sought and vigorously defended, even in the face of pressures for neighborhood change exerted by potential competitors for housing space. Certain ethnic or racial groups have had segregation forced on them.

 

-Gated Community- A subset of the master-planned community. A fenced or walled residential area with checkpoints staffed by security guards and access limited to designated individuals and identified guests.

 

-Institutional Controls- Have strongly influenced the land use arrangements and growth patterns of most US cities over the past century. Indeed the governments of most Western urbanized societies have instituted myriad laws to control all aspects of urban life with particular emphasis on the ways in which individual property and city areas can be developed and used. These nonmarket controls on land use are designed to minimize incompatibilities, provide for the creation in appropriate locations of public uses, and private uses needed for and conducive to a balanced, orderly community.

 

-Metropolitanization/Suburbanization- The two most prominent patterns of population growth between 1950 and 1990.

-Edge Cities- Regional and national headquarters of leading corporations, banking, professional services of all kinds, major hotel complexes and recreational centers-all formerly considered immovable keystones of central business districts-became part of the new outer cities. Sometimes called edge cities, they are defined by their large nodes of concentrated office and commercial structures and characterized by having more jobs than residents within their boundaries. Now exist in all regions of urbanized Anglo America.

-Brownfield Sites- Once vibrant industrial districts that have been left behind as blighted, polluted sites.

-Gentrification- Another part of central city residential revival is found in gentrification, the rehabilitation of housing tin the oldest and now deteriorated inner-city areas by middle and high-income groups. Welcomed by many as a positive, privately financed force in the renewal of depressed urban neighborhoods, gentrification also has serious negative social and housing impacts on the low-income, frequently minority families that are displaced. Another expression of the continuous remarking of urban land use and social patterns in accordance with the rent-paying abilities of alternate potential occupants.

-Inverse Concentric Zone- Pattern in which the elite and upper class reside in central areas and the social status declines with increasing distance from the center.

-Latin American City Model- At the center is the traditional market area, key government and religious buildings, and a modern central business district. Extending outward from the center is a commercial spine that features high status establishments and terminates at a suburban mall. Squatter settlements are found at the urban periphery and in disamenity zones such as near dumps, in flood-prone areas, or on slopes too steep for conventional development.

  2. Low-density development
  3. Leapfrog development miles beyond the urban fringe
  4. Fragmentation of political powers among many small municipalities
  5. Dominance of transportation by private automobiles
  6. Lack of centralized planning or control of land uses
  7. Commercial strip development
  8. Great fiscal disparities among towns and neighborhoods
  9. Segregation of types of land uses in different zones
  10. Reliance on a trickle-down process to provide low-income housing

 

Metropolitan Government (trait #6)- A regional governmental agency created to coordinate a variety of areawide functions such as water supply, transportation, open space, and waste management on behalf of the independent cities within the region. It is a response to the political fragmentation of the urban area.

Zoning- City planners traditionally use zoning to separate incompatible land uses, such as housing and chemical factories. Planning regulations that define permissible land uses for parcels of the city.

Congestion- Heavy traffic volumes exceeding the capacity of roads, causing travel delays.

Visual Preference Surveys- When shown photographs of urban scenery, most people prefer images of neighborhoods and shopping centers that do not reflect the designs typically built in sprawling cities.

New Urbanism- A popular alternative to a solution: a movement by architects and planners to build more traditional neighborhoods that foster a sense of community. New Urbanism emphasizes people, not cars.

Mixed-Use Developments (MUD)- A new kind of zoning classification, stresses location of residential and commercial uses in close proximity and at a greater density so that people can walk to work, stores, and schools. House types are also mixed to promote diversity. All of these alternative are seen as ways to promote neighborhood interaction instead of the sense of isolation that is characteristic of much of suburbia.

Form-Based Codes- A third and more recently proposed solution to sprawl. These are zoning regulations that focus on the form of development first and foremost-building placement, building type, lot configuration, and other elements of physical form. The intent is to balance predictability and flexibility-to regulate so that the community knows what it’s getting without constraining design too tightly.
Infill Development- Higher-density development in smaller patches of undeveloped or redevelopable land inside the urban boundaries.

Growth Boundaries- Instituted by Portland in 1980. Put fixed limits on urban expansion. A planning boundary beyond which conversion of rural land uses to urban land uses is strictly prohibited.

Light-Rail- Public transportation using rail at street level. Usually has a separate right-of-way on one side of a street or in a median. Unlike some streetcars and trolleys, light rail stops only at permanent stations. Operates at slower speeds, with lower capacities and costs, than subway, commuter rail, or elevated rail.

Transit-Oriented Development (TOD)- Dense nodes of housing, shops, restaurants, and offices accessible to light-rail or subway shops.

Stakeholders- An individual or group with a strong interest or stake in how an issue is decided. Antisprawl policies must satisfy them to be successful.

Scenarios- A sense of assumptions about the future that defines the basis for planning and modeling.

Ecologically Sensitive Space- Fragile, rare, or valuable natural areas that are good candidates for preservation as parks, wilderness, or open space.

Trade-Offs- One urban model will never fit all Americans. Geographers and planners refer to these types of situations as trade-offs, in which one objective must be sacrificed to achieve progress on another. A decision situation in which it is not possible to advance two conflicting goals simultaneously; that is, it is necessary to give up something in order to get something else. Compare with win-win situation.

Win-Win Situations- A solution in which it is possible to advance two goals simultaneously. Compare with trade-off.

Urban Village- A concentration of commercial land uses and higher-density housing outside the region’s main central business district that is planned to be a focal point of shopping and employment for surrounding residential areas. Can be the anchor point of an urban realm.The concept is loosely based on the principles of New Urbanism so that mixed-use development of homes, shops, entertainment, and work are nearby.

Infill- Fills in most existing developable space in the city before new growth occurs on the periphery. The result would be a denser inner city that minimizes the spatial extent of developed land into the rural plains.

Growth Boundary- One type of policy that can be used to achieve the infill strategy: literally, a line drawn on the map outside of which conversion of rural to urban land is prohibited. If such a line were drawn without including much available land, housing values would rapidly increase inside the boundary.



Chapter 12: The Political Ordering Of Space

-Political Geography- The study of the organization and distribution of political phenomena, including their impact on other spatial components of society and culture. Nationality is a basic element in cultural variation among people, and political geography traditionally has had a primary interest in country units, or states.

-State- On the international level, an independent political unit occupying a defined, permanently populated territory and having full sovereign control over its internal and foreign affairs.

-Nation- A group of people with a common culture occupying a particular territory, bound together by a strong sense of unity arising from shared beliefs and customs.

-Nation-State- Properly refers to a state whose territorial extent coincides with that occupied by a distinct nation or people or, at least, whose population shares a general sense of cohesion and adherence to a set of common values.

-Binational/Multinational State- A state that contains more than one nation. Often, no single ethnic group dominates the population.

-Part-Nation State- When a people’s sense of nationality exceeds the areal limits of a single country.

-Stateless Nation- A people without a state.

 

Challenges To The State:

  • The globalization of economies and the emergence of transnational corporations whose economic and production decisions are unrelated to the interests of any single state, including their home office state.
  • The proliferation of international and supranational institutions initially concerned with financial or security matters but all representing the voluntary surrender of some traditional state autonomy.
  • The emergence and multiplication of nongovernmental organizations (NGOs) whose specific interests and collective actions cut across national boundaries and unite people sharing common concerns about, for example, globalization, the environment, economic and social injustice, AIDS efforts, and the like.
  • The massive international migration flows that tend to undermine the state as a cultural community with assured and expected common values and loyalties.
  • The increase in nationalist and separatist movements in culturally composite states, weakening through demands for independence or regional autonomy the former unquestioned primacy of the established state.

 

-Compact State- The most efficient form assuming no major topographical barriers: a circle with the capital located in the center. In such a country, all places could be reached from the center in a minimal amount of time and with the least expenditure for roads, railway lines, and so on. It would also have the shortest possible borders to defend. Uruguay, Zimbabwe, and Poland have roughly circular shapes, forming a compact state.

-Prorupt States- Nearly compact but possess one or sometimes two narrow extensions of territory. Proruption may simply reflect peninsular elongations of land area, as in the case of Myanmar and Thailand.

-Elongated States- Least efficient shape administratively represented by countries like Norway, Vietnam, or Chile, which are long and narrow. The parts of the country far from the capital are likely to be isolated because great expenditures are required to link them to the core. Likely to encompass more diversity of climate, resources, and peoples.

-Fragmented States- Includes countries composed entirely of islands (Philippines, Indonesia), countries that are partly on islands and partly on the mainland (Italy, Malaysia), and those that are chiefly on the mainland but whose territory is separated by another state (US). Fragmentation makes it harder for the state to impose centralized control over its territory, particularly when the parts of the state are far from one another.

-Exclave- A special case of fragmentation occurs when a territorial outlier of one state, an exclave, is located within another country.

-Enclave- The counterpart of an exclave. Helps to define the fifth class of country shapes, the perforated state.

-Perforated State- Completely surrounds a territory that it does not rule as, for example, the Republic of South Africa surrounds Lesotho.

-Land-Locked State- Those lacking ocean frontage and surrounded by other states. Are at a commercial and strategic disadvantage.

-Core Area- Original nucleus of a state. Usually contains its most developed economic base, densest population and largest cities, and the most highly developed transportation systems.

-Unitary States- Countries with highly centralized governments, relatively few internal cultural contrasts, a strong sense of national identity, and borders that are clearly cultural as well as political boundaries.

-Federal States- In federal states, associations of more or less equal provinces or states with strong regional governmental responsibilities, the national capital city may have been newly created or selected to serve as the administrative center.

-Regional Government/Asymmetric Federalism- A new form of state organization; emerging in Europe as formerly strong unitary states acknowledge the autonomy aspirations of their several subdivisions and grant to them varying degrees of local administrative control while retaining in central hands authority over matters of nationwide concern, such as monetary policy, defense, foreign relations, and the like.

-Forward-Thrust Capital- A particular type of relocated capital; one that has been deliberately sited in a state’s interior to signal the government’s awareness of regions away from an off-center core and its interest in encouraging more uniform development.

-International Boundaries- Lines that establish the limit of each state’s jurisdiction and authority.

-Frontier Zones- Before boundaries were delimited, nations or empires were likely to be separated by frontier zones, ill-defined and fluctuating areas marking the effective end of a state’s authority.

-Natural (Physical) Boundaries- Those based on recognizable physiographic features, such as mountains, rivers, and lakes. Although they might seem to be attractive as borders because they actually exist in the landscape and are visible dividing elements, many natural boundaries have proved to be unsatisfactory.

-Geometric (Artificial) Boundaries- Frequently delimited as segments of parallels of latitude or meridians of longitude, they are found chiefly in Africa, Asia, and the Americans.

-Antecedent Boundary- One drawn across an area before it is well populated, that is, before most of the cultural landscape features were put in place.

-Subsequent- Boundaries drawn after the development of the cultural landscape.

-Consequent (Ethnographic)- One type of subsequent boundary, a border drawn to accommodate existing religious, linguistic, ethnic, or economic differences between countries.

-Superimposed Boundaries- Subsequent superimposed boundaries may also be forced on existing cultural landscapes, a country, or a people by a conquering or colonizing power that is unconcerned about pre existing cultural patterns.

-Relic Boundary- If a formal boundary line that no longer functions as such is still marked by some landscape features or differences on the two sides, it is termed a relic boundary.

 

Boundary Disputes:

 

  • Positional Disputes- occur when states disagree about the interpretation of documents that define a boundary and/or the way the boundary was delimited. Such disputes typically arise when the boundary is antecedent, preceding effective human settlement in the border region.
  • Territorial Disputes- Over the ownership of a region. Often, though not always, arise when a boundary that has been superimposed on the landscape divides an ethnically homogenous population. Conflicts can arise if the people of one state want to annex a territory whose population is ethnically related to that of the state but now subject to a foreign government. This type of expansionism is called irredentism and is often a consequence of superimposed boundaries.
  • Resource Disputes- Closely related to territorial conflicts. Neighboring states are likely to covet the resources--whether they be valuable mineral deposits, fertile farmland, or rich fishing grounds--lying in border areas and to disagree over their use.
  • Functional Disputes- Arise when neighboring states disagree over policies to be applied along a boundary. Such policies may concern immigration, the movement of traditionally nomadic groups, customs regulations, or land use.

 

 

-Centripetal Forces- Forces that promote unity and national stability in a state.

-Centrifugal Forces- Forces that destabilize and weaken a state.

-Nationalism- An identification with the state and the acceptance of national goals. Nationalism is based on the concept of allegiance to a single country and the ideals and the way of life it represents; it is an emotion that provides a sense of identity and loyalty and of collective distinction from all other peoples and lands. In contrast to its role as a powerful centripetal agency, is also a potentially disruptive centrifugal force.

-Iconography- The study of the symbols that help unite people. National anthems and other patriotic songs; flags, national sports teams, and officially designated or easily identified flowers and animals; and rituals and holidays are all developed by state to promote nationalism and attract allegiance.

-Organized Religion- Can be a potent centrifugal force. May compete with the state for people’s allegiance--one region the former USSR and other communist governments suppressed religion and promoted atheism.

-Subnationalism- Many people give their primary allegiance to traditional groups or nations that are smaller than the population of the entire state.

-Self-Determination- The right of a group to govern itself in its own state or territory.

-Separatism/Autonomous Nationalism- A dissident minority that has total or partial secession from the state as its primary goal is said to be guided by separatism or autonomous nationalism. In recent years, such nationalism has created currents of unrest within many countries, even long-established ones.

-Devolution- The transfer of some central powers to regional or local governments.

-Ethnic Cleansing- The killing or forcible relocation of less powerful minorities.

-Terrorism- The calculated use of violent acts against civilians and symbolic targets to publicize a cause, intimidate or coerce a civilian population, or affect the conduct of a government.

-International Terrorism- 9/11; include acts that transcend national boundaries. International terrorism is intended to intimidate people in other countries.

-Domestic Terrorism- Consists of acts by individuals or groups against the citizens or government of their own country.

-State Terrorism- Committed by the agents of a government.

-Subnational Terrorism- Committed by non-governmental groups.

 

Responses To Terrorist Acts:

  1. Reducing or addressing the causes of terrorism.
  2. Increasing international cooperation in the surveillance of subnational groups.
  3. Increasing security measures in a country.
  4. Using military means, either unilaterally or multilaterally, against terrorists or governments that sponsor terrorists.

 

-Nongovernmental Organization (NGOs)- Estimated at more than 20,000 in number and including such well-known groups as Greenpeace, Amnesty International, and Doctors without Borders. NGOs, through petitions, demonstrations, court actions, and educational efforts, have become effective influences on national and international political and economic actions.

-Supranationalism- Comprised of associations of three or more states created for mutual benefit and to achieve shared objectives.

-United Nationals Convention On The Law Of The Sea (UNCLOS)- Meeting over a period of years, delegates from more than 150 countries attempted to achieve consensus on a treaty that would establish an internationally agreed-upon “convention dealing with all matters relating to the Law Of The Sea.” The meetings culminated in a draft treaty in 1982, the UNCLOS. The convention delimits territorial boundaries and rights by defining four zones of diminishing control:

  • A territorial sea of up to 12 nm (19 km) in breadth over which coastal states have sovereignty, including exclusive fishing rights.
  • A contiguous zone to 24 nm (e8 km).
  • An exclusive economic zone (EEZ) of up to 200 nm (370 km) in which the state has recognized rights to explore, exploit, conserve, and manage the natural resources, both living and nonliving, of the seabed and waters.
  • The high seas beyond the EEZ.

 

-European Union (EU)- Grew out of the Common Market. It added new members slowly at first (Greece, Spain, Portugal in 1980s, Austria, Finland, and Sweden in 1995). As it gathered momentum, more countries were admitted to the EU during the early 200s. It is now the world’s largest and richest bloc of nation-states.

-Military Alliances- Based on the principle that unity assures strength. Such pacts usually provide for mutual assistance in the case of aggression.

-Political Alliances- More generalized mutual concerns or appeals to historical interest may be the basis for primarily political alliances. Such associations tend to be rather loose, not requiring their members to yield much power to the union. (Commonwealth of Nations, Organization Of American States): both offer economic as well as political benefits.

-Electoral Geography- One aspect is the analysis of how boundaries are drawn around voting districts. Also addresses the spatial patterns yielded by election results and their relationship to the socioeconomic characteristics of voters.

-Gerrymandering- The practice of drawing the boundaries of voting districts so as to unfairly favor one political party over another, to fragment voting blocs, or to achieve other nondemocratic objectives.

-Stacked Gerrymandering- Involves drawing circuitous boundaries to enclose pockets of strength or weakness of the group in power.

-Excess Vote- Concentrates the votes of the opposition in a few districts, which they can win easily, but leaves them few potential seats elsewhere.

-Wasted Vote- Dilutes the opposition’s strength by dividing its votes among the number of districts.
